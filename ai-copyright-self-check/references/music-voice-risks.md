# Music / BGM / Voice Risk

Use this reference for videos, ads, AI covers, voice cloning, platform music libraries, soundalikes, and any work with audio.

## Music Rights Are Layered

A track may involve:
- Composition rights: melody and lyrics.
- Sound recording rights: the specific recording/master.
- Performance rights.
- Synchronization use: pairing music with video.
- Platform-specific library terms.

Getting one layer does not automatically clear the others.

## AI Music Is Not a Laundering Tool

Treat generated music as high-risk when it is created from unlicensed source music, even if the final file was generated by Suno, Udio, or another AI music tool.

Important rule:

```text
If the creator used an unlicensed song as training, distillation, melody reference, stem source, chord map, cover input, remix input, or close A/B target, and the output is recognizably similar to that song, do not rate it Green.
```

This includes attempts to avoid explicit platform checks by not typing artist names or song titles, while still reconstructing:
- the chorus hook;
- the verse or pre-chorus melody;
- a signature riff, intro, drop, rhythm pattern, or bass line;
- lyrics, rhyme scheme, slogan, or memorable phrase;
- the singer's tone, phrasing, ad-libs, vibrato, breath, or performance identity;
- the original song's arrangement map, emotional arc, or section order.

Suno paid-plan commercial rights solve only the relationship between the user and Suno for eligible generated songs. They do not prove that the output is free from third-party similarity claims, and they do not cure an unlicensed source-song workflow.

## Source-Laundering Questions

Ask these whenever the user mentions Suno, Udio, AI music, covers, remixes, or "I made something similar":

- Was a Bilibili, NetEase Cloud Music, QQ Music, YouTube, Spotify, TikTok, or local MP3 track used as a reference?
- Did the user upload original audio, stems, vocals, accompaniment, MIDI, melody, chord charts, or transcribed sections into any tool?
- Did the user first extract melody/chords/BPM/structure from a copyrighted song and then ask Suno to recreate the feeling?
- Did the user intentionally avoid writing a famous song or artist name while describing enough details to reproduce it?
- Does a normal listener identify the result as a copy, parody, soundalike, or "AI version" of a specific song within 5-10 seconds?
- Is the output being used for sponsored content, ads, courses, client delivery, distribution, or monetized accounts?

## Common Red Flags

Rate Red when:
- A commercial/sponsored/client video uses a popular song without a clear commercial sync license.
- The user uses a platform trending sound outside that platform or in a paid ad.
- AI music was generated from an unlicensed existing song, stem, melody extraction, chord transcription, cover/remix input, or distillation workflow.
- The output is clearly recognizable as a soundalike or close remake of a specific song, even if no artist name or title appears in the prompt.
- A key section such as chorus, hook, intro riff, drop, or vocal phrase is substantially similar to a known song and the use is commercial or promotional.
- AI cloning imitates a known singer, actor, creator, or celebrity voice.
- AI cover content uses a recognizable song and recognizable celebrity voice.
- A voice model was trained from someone else's voice without permission.
- The user cannot identify the BGM source for a client deliverable.

## Common Yellow Flags

Rate Yellow when:
- A platform music library is used, but the post is sponsored or cross-posted.
- The user has "royalty-free" music but the license does not clearly cover ads, paid courses, or client use.
- The music is AI-generated but sounds like a famous song, artist, or hook.
- The user says the music was "inspired by" or "based on" a specific song, but cannot provide a clean-room workflow or license.
- Audio tools do not identify a match, but ordinary listeners still point to the same specific source song.
- The voice is "inspired by" a famous performer, even if not named.

## Practical Soundalike Review

Use tools only as supporting evidence. A negative match is not a safe result.

Recommended check:

1. Preserve the evidence chain: prompt, generation date, account tier, lyrics source, uploaded audio, reference files, edits, and export history.
2. Run ordinary-listener test: play 10-20 seconds of the hook, chorus, intro, and drop. If several listeners name the same source song, escalate.
3. Run A/B comparison if a source is suspected: compare melody contour, rhythm, chord timing, section structure, signature riff, lyrics, vocal phrasing, and production ear-candy.
4. Use recognition tools when available: Shazam, SoundHound, YouTube Content ID private upload, ACRCloud, AudD, AcoustID/Chromaprint, or distributor pre-checks.
5. If the result is for ads, sponsorships, client delivery, paid courses, or music distribution, require cleaner provenance or replacement even when tools find no match.

Do not say "no infringement detected" for music unless the evidence is strong. Prefer:

```text
No obvious match was found from the available checks, but music similarity can still create risk. Keep the source records, avoid commercial use if the hook is close to a known song, and replace it if ordinary listeners identify a specific source.
```

## Safer Options

Recommend:
- Use explicitly commercial-licensed music.
- Use original commissioned or self-generated music with terms saved.
- Keep platform-library music inside the platform and inside allowed use.
- For Suno/Udio/AI music, start from abstract mood, tempo, instrumentation, and scene function instead of a specific song, singer, album, or hidden reference track.
- If the user wants "that song's feeling," translate it into neutral direction: tempo range, mood, instrumentation, vocal gender, energy curve, and production density; do not preserve melody, hook, lyrics, arrangement, or signature sound.
- Replace celebrity voice with a generic original voice.
- Store license screenshots, receipts, terms, and project files.

## Audio Review Limits

Do not claim certain identification unless the user provides a track name, audio/video file, platform link, or reliable metadata. If identification is uncertain, say so and recommend conservative replacement or licensing.

Music checks have a hard limit: exact audio fingerprint tools may miss AI-generated soundalikes because the recording is new while the protected similarity may sit in melody, composition, arrangement, or performance imitation. Treat "tool did not match" as weaker than "listeners can clearly identify the source."

For legal/publication claims about active music lawsuits, recent settlements, platform terms, or statutory rules, verify current sources first.
